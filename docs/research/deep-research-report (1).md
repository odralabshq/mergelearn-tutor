## Best evidence found

The most relevant literature breaks into two tiers. The first tier is **indirect but strong**: decades of research on retrieval practice, spacing, self-explanation, worked examples, and intelligent tutoring. The second tier is **direct but thinner**: recent studies of code review comprehension, debugging in professional practice, GenAI-assisted programming, and overreliance in programming education. Taken together, these literatures support MergeLearnŌĆÖs *direction* more than they validate its exact implementation. **Roediger and Butler on retrieval practice.**  
**Study type:** review of retrieval-practice literature. **Population:** general learners. **Intervention:** testing/retrieval practice versus restudy. **Outcomes:** long-term retention and transfer. **Strength:** very strong general evidence. **Applicability:** **indirectly applicable**. The central finding is that retrieving information from memory improves later retention more than passive restudy, especially when retrieval is effortful and successful. For MergeLearn, that strongly supports answer-first cards and argues against explanation-first UX. The caveat is that the original evidence is not specific to source-code comprehension or professionals. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28search0Ņłéturn28search7Ņłü

**Cepeda and colleagues on distributed practice.**  
**Study type:** review and quantitative synthesis. **Population:** general learners. **Intervention:** spaced versus massed practice. **Outcomes:** verbal recall and long-term retention. **Strength:** very strong general evidence. **Applicability:** **indirectly applicable**. This literature strongly supports a scheduler that revisits concepts over intervals rather than a one-off ŌĆ£review todayŌĆÖs changesŌĆØ loop. For MergeLearn, the implication is that recently changed files are useful as seeds, but learning gains will likely depend on later review of the same concepts under delayed conditions. ## Missing proof

A second unproven claim is that **repo-specific provenance and graph views improve metacognition or debugging of understanding**. They may increase trust, transparency, and debugging of the tutor itself, which is valuable, but the literature I found does not provide strong direct evidence that timeline or graph visualisations increase retention or transfer in programming. A third missing proof concerns **professionals rather than students**. ## Evaluation plan

**Hypotheses.**  
The most defensible first hypothesis is: **developers using MergeLearn will score higher on delayed repo-specific comprehension and transfer tasks than developers who only reread the diff or consume an AI-generated explanation**. The second is: **answer-before-reveal will outperform reveal-first or explanation-first flows on delayed performance**. The third is: **spaced revisit schedules will outperform same-session cram review**. The fourth is: **confidence calibration will improve when confidence judgments are captured explicitly and contrasted with correctness over time**. The fifth is: **repo-grounded questions will beat generic programming flashcards on relevance and transfer for developers working in a specific codebase**. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn28search0Ņłéturn24search1Ņłéturn8academia5Ņłéturn9academia2Ņłéturn12academia1Ņłü

**Metrics.**  
Use at least five outcome families: **comprehension**, **retention**, **transfer**, **metacognitive calibration**, and **workflow value**. Concretely, that means delayed comprehension quizzes on changed code; transfer tasks such as predicting failing tests, locating blast radius, or explaining why a fix works; time-to-correctness on debugging or code-review tasks; confidence-versus-accuracy calibration metrics such as Brier score or calibration curves; and user-level workflow outcomes such as whether the tool changed review decisions, surfaced misunderstandings, or reduced unverified AI acceptance.
