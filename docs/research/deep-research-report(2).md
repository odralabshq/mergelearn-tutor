# MergeLearn Tutor Product Experiment and Evaluation Design

## Executive answer

The platform design is **plausible and worth evaluating**, but the current evidence base supports only a **qualified** claim: MergeLearn Tutor is built around learning mechanisms that are generally strong in cognitive science and directionally promising in programming education, yet it has **not** proved that those mechanisms transfer cleanly to professional developers reviewing their own AI-assisted code changes. Retrieval practice typically outperforms passive restudy for longer-term retention, and prompting people to explain their own work can reveal fragile understanding; however, recent software-engineering studies also show that AI-assisted development shifts work from writing to verification, can create review overhead, and can undermine skill formation if users delegate too much cognition to the model. That means MergeLearn should be evaluated as a **trust-calibration and knowledge-debt reduction tool**, not as a generic productivity booster. citeturn11news0turn11academia1turn15academia0turn15academia3turn3academia2

Confidence is **medium**. The strongest support is for the underlying learning primitives: active recall, delayed review, self-explanation, and confidence calibration. The weakest support is for the product-specific leap from those primitives to “better software engineering outcomes in live repositories”, because that requires domain-specific field evidence that the product does not yet have. The product’s own sources show that MergeLearn already has the right scaffolding for such a programme: local-first evidence ingestion, answer-first review, reveal-then-self-grade, provenance inspection, card-quality gates, correction loops, and an evaluation harness. But the existing harness measures grounding, answerability, and manual usefulness, not comprehension, retention, transfer, or calibrated review judgement. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file4 fileciteturn0file5 fileciteturn0file6 fileciteturn0file7

The five most evidence-backed design implications are these. First, the primary outcome should be **delayed code comprehension**, not immediate satisfaction, because retrieval effects are most meaningful after delay and self-ratings can be misleading. Second, every meaningful study should use an **active control**, especially “re-read diff/docs” or “read the packet passively”, because the real competitor is not no learning at all; it is the existing verification workflow. Third, MergeLearn should explicitly measure **confidence calibration** alongside correctness, because recent human-AI work shows that confidence cues can affect trust and misuse, and recent software studies suggest the central problem in AI-generated multi-file review is trust calibration rather than raw diffing. Fourth, the product should separate **content-quality metrics** from **learning-outcome metrics**: a card can be grounded yet unhelpful, or effortful yet genuinely educational. Fifth, claims about productivity, bug reduction, and skill preservation should be withheld until field evidence is available, because recent studies cut both ways and show context-sensitive trade-offs rather than a simple “AI makes development faster” story. citeturn11news0turn14academia7turn3academia2turn3academia3turn15academia0turn15academia1turn7academia4turn15academia3

## Best evidence found

The most relevant evidence cluster is the learning-science foundation. A well-known experiment comparing retrieval practice against elaborative studying with concept mapping found that retrieval-based studying improved later performance, even though alternative strategies often produced higher feelings of understanding. That distinction matters a great deal for MergeLearn, because developers may *feel* that re-reading a diff or an AI explanation was enough when it was not. The product should therefore treat “felt usefulness” as a secondary measure rather than a proof of learning. citeturn11news0turn11search2

The retained-learning case also supports delayed review. Research on spaced repetition and adaptive scheduling shows that retention depends strongly on the spacing and timing of review, and later educational-data-mining work suggests that models that incorporate forgetting and skill history can outperform simpler schedulers. This does not prove that a specific spaced schedule will work for repository-specific code knowledge, but it strongly supports building delayed-recall instrumentation and testing at least a few review intervals rather than relying only on same-session card success. citeturn4academia1turn4academia3

For programming-specific justification, the best evidence is not that flashcards teach syntax; it is that people often cannot explain code they have already written. A study of questions about students’ own programs found that about one third of students struggled to explain their own code, and performance on those aligned questions correlated more strongly with success and retention than merely submitting a correct program. A companion line of work explicitly argued for automatically generating questions about a learner’s own code after functional success, precisely because passing tests does not guarantee understanding. MergeLearn’s thesis maps closely onto this idea, except aimed at AI-assisted repository work rather than novice coursework. The caveat is that these studies are mainly educational rather than professional settings, so external validity to experienced developers must be tested rather than assumed. citeturn11academia1turn9academia3

The self-explanation case is encouraging but mixed. A recent experiment in calculus found that open-ended self-explanation with LLM feedback improved some transfer-oriented explanation outcomes, even when learners completed fewer practice problems, but did not produce broad across-the-board post-test gains. That is a useful warning for MergeLearn: active recall and self-explanation may improve explanation quality, transfer, or metacognition before they improve raw accuracy on every near-term test. The product should therefore include open-ended explanation measures and not reduce evaluation to multiple-choice correctness alone. citeturn16academia0

The strongest AI-era motivation comes from recent software-engineering work showing that AI changes the shape of work. An RCT with Google software engineers found a time-on-task reduction of about 21% for one enterprise task setting, while the METR RCT on experienced open-source developers found the opposite: AI use increased completion time by 19% in familiar codebases. A 2026 longitudinal study of professional engineers found that AI coding assistants shifted work from creation toward verification and “supervisory engineering work,” while perceived productivity gains coexisted with worsening aspects of developer experience. Together, these studies argue against simple speed claims and in favour of measuring review effort, debugging burden, and calibration directly. citeturn15academia1turn7academia4turn15academia3

The clearest product-adjacent HCI result is that review of LLM-generated multi-file changes is, fundamentally, a **trust-calibration problem**. In the recent participatory JetBrains study, practitioners centred trust calibration as the key challenge and preferred a three-level review workflow with risk and confidence signals. Industry studies of automated code review also report that AI comments can be useful and often acted upon, but they may lengthen pull-request closure times, and their effectiveness depends on qualities such as concision, snippet grounding, and hunk-level relevance. That aligns well with MergeLearn’s current emphasis on snippet-first cards, evidence linkage, and provenance; it also means evaluation should measure review quality and reviewer confidence, not only whether users “liked” the cards. citeturn3academia2turn3academia3turn6academia6

The evidence on overreliance and skill formation is especially important for claims about “knowledge debt”. Anthropic researchers found that AI assistance, in their study of learning a new asynchronous library, impaired conceptual understanding, code reading, and debugging ability without delivering significant average efficiency gains; the harmful cases were those where participants fully delegated cognition rather than using the model in more engaged ways. More general human-AI calibration research likewise shows that miscalibrated confidence can induce misuse of overconfident systems and disuse of underconfident ones. This gives MergeLearn a concrete falsifiable claim: it can succeed if it **improves correctness-conditioned confidence** and reduces unearned confidence when reviewing AI-assisted changes. citeturn15academia0turn14academia7turn14academia2

Finally, existing MergeLearn sources reveal both strengths and gaps. The product already supports local-first review, active recall with “answer first, reveal explanation, self-grade,” provenance via Timeline and Graph, and a correction loop that distinguishes “bad card” or “wrong evidence” from learner failure. The current evaluation harness, however, is focused on grounded concept rate, heuristic answerability, readiness verdicts, and manual usefulness ratings. Those are worth keeping, but they are **content pipeline** metrics, not proof that the tutor improves comprehension, retention, transfer, or calibrated review behaviour. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file5 fileciteturn0file6 fileciteturn0file7

## Missing proof

Several claims are currently unproven and should be treated that way in product copy and investor conversations. There is no evidence yet that MergeLearn Tutor improves **professional developers’ comprehension of their own recent AI-assisted changes**, because the current harness does not test comprehension outcomes. There is also no evidence yet that it improves **retention after delay**, **transfer to nearby bugs or features**, **debugging speed or success**, or **confidence calibration** during PR or change review. What exists today is good evidence that the system can produce grounded and inspectable study material locally, and directional evidence from adjacent research that such material *could* improve learning if the questions are well-designed. fileciteturn0file7 citeturn11academia1turn16academia0

The product should also avoid claiming that it reduces AI overreliance until it can show either behavioural change or better calibration. Recent research shows that AI assistance can either help or hurt depending on task context, prior expertise, and whether people remain cognitively engaged. Likewise, “AI review support” can improve quality while also increasing closure time or noise. Those are not contradictions; they mean the product must measure trade-offs. citeturn15academia0turn15academia1turn7academia4turn3academia3

From a proof-design standpoint, sceptical audiences will ask different questions. Developers will ask whether the cards are answerable, specific, and worth the annoyance. Engineering managers will ask whether the tool improves understanding without slowing teams down or exposing code. Researchers will ask whether the study design separates card quality from actual learning and whether delayed tests, rubrics, and grading are valid. Buyers and investors will ask whether the same local-first, evidence-grounded workflow drives both user value and sticky adoption. MergeLearn has credible answers to the privacy and evidence-grounding questions already, but not yet to the learning-effectiveness questions. fileciteturn0file4 fileciteturn0file5 fileciteturn0file6 citeturn3academia2turn0academia0

## Evaluation plan

The right evaluation programme has to separate three layers that are easy to conflate: **content quality**, **learning effectiveness**, and **workflow value**. Content quality asks whether cards and explanations are grounded, answerable, and non-noisy. Learning effectiveness asks whether users actually understand and retain more. Workflow value asks whether the product fits into modern AI-assisted development without imposing unreasonable friction. MergeLearn already has partial coverage for the first layer. The research plan below fills the second and third. fileciteturn0file7

The core hypotheses should be explicit. The first is that developers using MergeLearn Tutor after an AI-assisted change will score higher on **immediate comprehension** of that change than developers who spend the same time re-reading the diff, docs, or commit summary. The second is that they will retain more after **two days and seven days**. The third is that they will transfer better to a **nearby bug or feature task** in the same area of the repo. The fourth is that they will show better **debugging performance** when presented with a bug related to the reviewed change. The fifth is that their **confidence will be better calibrated** to correctness. The sixth is that the tool will reduce **AI overreliance behaviours**, such as accepting an AI-authored change with high confidence but low evidence inspection. The seventh is that the system can achieve acceptable **useful-card rate** and **product retention** without needing remote telemetry or remote LLM calls by default. These hypotheses flow directly from the product brief and current workflow. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file5

The primary metrics should be concrete and pre-specified:

| Outcome | Primary metric | Supporting metrics | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Code comprehension | Proportion correct on repo-grounded quiz items | Open-ended explanation rubric, code-tracing accuracy, file-role accuracy | Use multiple item types to avoid rewarding rote recall only. |
| Retention after delay | Same comprehension score at 2-day and 7-day delay | Retention ratio relative to immediate score; survival of mastered concepts | A 14-day probe is useful in beta, but 2- and 7-day are enough for MVP. |
| Transfer | Score on new but nearby task or unseen snippet in same concept area | Explanation quality on transfer item; need-for-hints | Distinguish near transfer from far transfer. |
| Debugging performance | Time to correct bug localisation and time to correct fix | Number of false hypotheses, number of navigation hops, final correctness | If execution is impractical, use localisation + patch-selection tasks. |
| Review confidence calibration | Absolute calibration gap, Brier score, or ECE over confidence judgements | Overconfidence gap, underconfidence gap, confidence-conditioned accuracy | Confidence should be logged before answer reveal. |
| AI-overreliance reduction | Rate of unjustified high-confidence acceptance of wrong AI-authored changes | Evidence-inspection rate, reveal-before-attempt rate, blind trust rate | Needs explicit AI-authored review tasks. |
| Useful-card rate | Share of cards rated answerable and useful at 4 or 5 out of 5, without wrong-evidence flag | Reveal-before-attempt, skip rate, correction events | This remains a product-health metric, not the main outcome. |
| Adoption and retention | Weekly active users among opt-ins, sessions per week, median cards reviewed per active week | Week-4 retention, week-8 retention, course completion | Interpret with caution in forced studies. |

The outcome tasks should be grounded in repo evidence rather than generic programming quizzes. The immediate and delayed tests should draw from the same families of questions that MergeLearn already supports or could support: “explain what changed here”, “trace what happens for this input”, “which test matters most and why”, “which file is highest risk and why”, and “what adjacent file or concept would you inspect next”. Each item should be tagged by concept, evidence key, question plane, and difficulty, so that performance can be analysed by content type rather than only in aggregate. Because code understanding is multi-faceted and the software-engineering literature uses diverse measures, a composite score is better than any single proxy. fileciteturn0file6 fileciteturn0file7 citeturn8academia1

The minimum feasible experiment for internal dogfooding is a **randomised within-subject crossover** over real, recent changes. Each participant should review a stream of changes for which they have some plausible stake or familiarity. Half of those changes should be assigned to the MergeLearn condition; half to an active control condition where the participant spends the same amount of time re-reading the diff, docs, and commit message. Immediate tests should follow right after review; delayed tests should appear after two days and seven days. Because the same person serves as their own control, this design reduces noise from skill, repo familiarity, and language differences. As a rough planning estimate, a paired design usually needs about **34 completed participants** to detect a medium effect of *d*≈0.5 with 80% power and two-sided α=.05; if effects are more modest, around *d*≈0.4, a safer target is roughly **50–55 completed participants**. Those are planning estimates rather than claims about what the true effect size will be. citeturn15academia1turn7academia4turn15academia3

A second MVP-feasible experiment is a **single-user or tiny-team alternating-treatments study**. This is not publishable on its own, but it is very useful for falsification. The same user alternates by day or by change between MergeLearn and active-control review for four to six weeks. The goal is not p-values; it is to see whether the product can consistently produce answerable cards, delayed-recall completions, and any visible lift on own-code comprehension without causing users to abandon the workflow. In this phase, the key decision gates are practical: delayed probe completion rate, wrong-evidence rate, useful-card rate, and directional calibration improvement. If the system cannot survive this dogfood phase, larger studies will mostly measure noise. fileciteturn0file7

The best controlled “proof of mechanism” study is a **programming-task experiment with seeded or curated changes**. Participants should first receive a repository packet describing a recent change. They then either interact with MergeLearn or study the same materials passively for a matched duration. Afterwards they complete a comprehension test, a transfer task, and a debugging or localisation task. This can be done on internal benchmark repos or public fixtures, but the tasks must be independently graded and should include both AI-authored and human-authored change variants so the team can test whether MergeLearn is especially helpful when reviewing AI-generated changes. A between-subject design is possible here, but for moderate effects it is expensive: as a rough planning estimate, about **64 participants per arm** are needed for *d*≈0.5 and about **100 per arm** for *d*≈0.4. Unless recruitment is abundant, a crossover design remains much more realistic. citeturn15academia1turn7academia4

The strongest beta-stage field evidence would come from a **professional-developer field study** lasting four to eight weeks. Participants would use the product on real repository work with delayed probes on a subset of changes. The main outcomes would be delayed comprehension, confidence calibration, and workflow adoption. A sensible design is partial randomisation at the change level: for each participant, some recent changes are assigned to MergeLearn and some to control review. That avoids forcing an entire team to surrender its habitual workflow. It also suits a local-first product because raw code never needs to leave the device; only opt-in, code-free event exports need to be analysed centrally. This is the phase where managers start caring about review friction, session length, and whether the tool creates net value in a verification-heavy AI workflow. fileciteturn0file4 citeturn15academia3turn3academia2turn3academia3

Instrumentation must be added deliberately and conservatively. At minimum, the product should log local events for review start, card impression, answer submission, confidence before reveal, reveal timestamp, self-grade, usefulness rating, answerability rating, correction taxonomy event, evidence inspection event, delayed-probe schedule, delayed-probe completion, and optional debugging-task start and finish. Each event should reference **hashed repo ID**, **concept ID**, **evidence key**, **question plane**, **item type**, **difficulty bucket**, and **session ID**, but should not include raw source or snippets in analytics exports. Because the product’s current privacy model emphasises no telemetry and inspectable local state, exports should be explicit, local, and human-reviewable before sharing. fileciteturn0file0 fileciteturn0file4 fileciteturn0file5

The product should add four instrumented task families. The first is a **pre/post quiz** family for immediate comprehension and delayed retention. The second is a **calibration family** requiring users to rate confidence on a 0–100 or 1–5 scale *before* reveal or grading. The third is a **code-tracing and localisation family**, because understanding is not just knowing a definition; it is being able to reason through behaviour and find what matters. The fourth is a **transfer family**, where a later task changes the surface form while preserving the underlying concept. MergeLearn should also record a **reveal-without-attempt** event because it is a useful proxy for cards that are too hard, too vague, or too noisy. citeturn8academia1turn8academia2turn11academia1turn16academia0

Two quality-control instruments are especially important. The first is **evidence coverage and hallucination audit**. A random sample of cards should be manually or independently checked for whether the prompt and explanation are actually supported by the cited evidence. Recent work on hallucination detection in AI code review is relevant here: groundedness is a real failure mode, and it should be measured separately from learning. The second is **blind answer grading**. For open-ended explanation items, early studies should use human raters with a short rubric and inter-rater agreement checks. Do not let the same generative model both produce cards and grade them without calibration and audit; current grading-calibration research shows that confidence and reliability need explicit checking even for LLM graders. citeturn0academia0turn13academia2

The “minimum viable proof package” should differ by audience. A sceptical developer is most likely to be persuaded by a small but clean result: for example, a within-subject comparison showing that after reviewing their own recent changes, they answer more delayed questions correctly and are less overconfident than when they just re-read a diff. An engineering manager will want to see that usefulness is high, wrong-evidence is low, delayed comprehension improves, and the workflow does not inflate review time catastrophically. A CS education researcher will want a preregistered design, delayed tests, open rubrics, blinded grading, and a clear distinction between content quality and learning outcomes. An investor or buyer will want the above distilled into a simple story: developers are becoming supervisors of AI-generated code, MergeLearn addresses that bottleneck, and early studies show both behavioural value and learnable retention without compromising privacy. citeturn15academia3turn3academia2turn7academia9turn15academia0

The product should avoid several claims until the studies support them. It should not claim that it **improves software delivery speed**, because the current AI productivity literature is mixed and highly context-dependent. It should not claim that it **reduces defects**, **reduces incidents**, or **improves code quality** unless those are measured directly in field deployments. It should not claim that it **prevents skill erosion** or **reduces AI overreliance** until behavioural evidence exists. It should not claim that its spaced-review loop is “scientifically proven for code” until delayed-recall studies in the product demonstrate that. And it should not collapse groundedness into learning: a grounded card may still be a weak teaching intervention, while a highly rated card may still fail to improve retention. citeturn15academia0turn15academia1turn7academia4turn3academia3

## Phased evaluation roadmap

The **MVP proof** phase should answer only one question: *does the product create grounded, answerable, delay-worthy learning events over real recent changes?* The right studies are internal dogfood and small beta-like within-subject trials. Success here means useful-card rate above a clear threshold, low wrong-evidence rate, acceptable delayed-probe completion, and at least directional improvement on delayed own-code comprehension and calibration versus passive re-reading. Failure at this stage should stop larger claims, because it would show that the mechanism is not yet working even in favourable conditions. fileciteturn0file7 citeturn11academia1turn15academia0

The **beta proof** phase should answer a stronger question: *does MergeLearn outperform the current review habit in realistic repository work, without unacceptable workflow cost?* This is where four-to-eight-week professional field studies belong, with change-level randomisation, delayed probes, calibration metrics, and active-control comparisons. The goal is not to prove lower incident rates yet; the goal is to demonstrate a believable pattern that developers are learning more durable, better calibrated knowledge in the exact verification-heavy workflow that AI coding assistants now create. Evidence of stable adoption, repeated voluntary use, and positive delayed-learning effects would be enough to justify continued product investment. citeturn15academia3turn3academia2turn7academia9

The **publishable study proof** phase should answer the scientific version of the product hypothesis: *does grounded active recall over one’s own recent code changes reduce knowledge debt better than passive review, and under what conditions?* That phase should be preregistered, use validated rubrics, include delayed retention and transfer tasks, report calibration alongside accuracy, and separate content-quality covariates from learning outcomes. Ideally it should include at least one controlled repo-task study and one longitudinal field component, with open materials and anonymised analysis scripts.
